Documentation

I used a lot in this project!
Audience: Anyone looking for something to waste some time and possible employers.
Goal: Create 3 “scenes” to convey different feelings, but with the same art style and “feeling” in terms of interactivity. My ultimate goal is to convey a moment in time instead of show something fancy. I want it to convey my personal art style while retaining the functionality that makes it simple and satisfying to use.
How/When/Why: Ideally, they’d come upon my website through looking at my portfolio. I’d also consider what I wanted to make as a “game”, so maybe if they were looking for things on itch.io they could find my graphics! I hope they would find my site when they look at my portfolio, or want to look at my web development skills for a co-op.
I wanted this website to feel sketchy, messy, and exactly reflect my art style (quick doodles on the corners of notes). I also wanted it to be casual, which I think is reflected in the typeface choices. I don't really want to give the impression that anything is serious or too stiff. Web design is fun! All my colors were meant to be dreamy-cute and fun. I think pink is a great color!

I used (in no particular order):

  • Everything that the PixiJS tutorials/examples/documentation folks offer on their website, but particularly the tutorial by kittykatattack. Almost all of that is verbatim from her tutorial, with variable names changed, just because I hadn't used Pixi before.
  • Stuff on object-fit I got from StackOverflow and MDN.
  • SVG styling help from StackOverflow (mostly the fact that you can do CSS styling with jQuery on SVGs).
  • The jQuery docs themselves!
  • An extreme amount of w3schools and MDN, especially for scroll-behavior.

In terms of above and beyond, most of my stuff came from just learning the PixiJS docs. I spent about 4 hours learning about renderers and everything that Pixi is built on and used a jQuery offset to place the immensely large canvas so it wouldn't start on the first page. Besides that, I think the horizontal site and the cool scroll behavior stuff is interesting. I thought about having "one" site with all the graphics immensely off the page then flying in with CSS transitions, but ultimately scroll-behavior and id tags were way easier, which I didn't know about before!
There were a lot of things I struggled with on this project. I really didn't understand jQuery as much as I thought I did, and this project really pushed my limits on understanding how jQuery actually works. I also got a very quick lesson on how pixiJS handles multiple canvases (it doesn't), so I really had to figure out how to recover from a lot of lost time.
